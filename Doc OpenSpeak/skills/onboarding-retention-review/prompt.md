# Onboarding & Retention Review Skill

## Purpose

Evaluate whether the application can successfully:
- communicate its value quickly;
- create emotional connection;
- establish trust;
- encourage healthy long-term usage;
- support sustainable retention.

This review must analyze not only onboarding usability, but also:
- emotional onboarding;
- habit formation;
- perceived value;
- ritual creation;
- long-term attachment potential;
- retention psychology.

The objective is to determine whether the product can realistically sustain:
- D1;
- D7;
- D30;
- D365 retention;

without relying on manipulative engagement mechanics.

The review should aggressively identify:
- friction;
- emotional disconnect;
- habit-breaking UX;
- feature fatigue;
- premature complexity;
- trust erosion;
- addictive mechanics disguised as wellbeing.

# Core Review Philosophy

Healthy retention does not come from:
- addiction loops;
- guilt;
- pressure;
- compulsive usage;
- engagement maximization.

Healthy retention comes from:
- emotional value;
- calm rituals;
- trust;
- perceived usefulness;
- emotional safety;
- personal meaning;
- habit integration.

The application should feel:
- supportive;
not demanding.

The review must protect the emotional integrity of the product.

---

# Core Evaluation Areas

## 1. First Impression Quality

Evaluate:
- immediate clarity;
- emotional tone;
- trustworthiness;
- perceived quality;
- calmness;
- focus.

Review:
- launch experience;
- first screen;
- initial emotional impact;
- visual confidence;
- cognitive load.

Detect:
- overwhelming first impression;
- visual noise;
- confusing purpose;
- weak differentiation;
- generic onboarding patterns;
- immediate friction.

The first seconds should create clarity and emotional confidence.

---

## 2. Value Proposition Clarity

Evaluate whether users quickly understand:
- what the product does;
- why it matters;
- why it is different;
- why they should return.

Detect:
- vague positioning;
- abstract explanations;
- unclear benefits;
- feature-first communication;
- excessive explanation;
- weak emotional framing.

The value should feel intuitive, not explained through paragraphs.

---

## 3. Onboarding Friction

Evaluate:
- onboarding length;
- required effort;
- account creation pressure;
- permission timing;
- cognitive load;
- setup complexity.

Detect:
- excessive onboarding steps;
- permission abuse;
- forced registration;
- premature commitment;
- unnecessary customization;
- too many choices;
- “tutorial fatigue”.

The onboarding should reduce anxiety, not create work.

---

## 4. Emotional Connection

Evaluate whether the onboarding creates:
- calmness;
- trust;
- emotional resonance;
- feeling of care;
- feeling of intentionality.

Detect:
- emotionally cold UX;
- productivity-app feeling;
- transactional onboarding;
- generic wellness clichés;
- emotionally empty interactions.

The user should feel:
- welcomed;
not processed.

---

## 5. Habit Formation Potential

Evaluate whether the app can realistically become:
- a ritual;
- a routine;
- a calm recurring interaction.

Review:
- usage frequency expectations;
- interaction effort;
- emotional payoff;
- repeatability;
- daily integration.

Detect:
- high-friction recurring flows;
- novelty-only engagement;
- shallow loops;
- interactions that feel like chores;
- weak recurring value.

Retention should emerge naturally from meaningful repetition.

---

## 6. D1 / D7 / D30 / D365 Retention Potential

- immediate value delivery;
- emotional reward;
- first-session satisfaction;

Detect:

- confusion after onboarding.

---

### D7 Retention

Evaluate:

Detect:

- novelty decay;

---

### D30 Retention

Evaluate:
- long-term emotional sustainability;
- ritual strength;
- meaningful accumulation;
- attachment potential.

Detect:

- shallow interaction depth.

---

### D365 Retention

Evaluate:
- identity integration;
- emotional trust;
- lifestyle compatibility;
- sustainable calmness.

Detect:
- burnout risk;
- feature fatigue;
- compulsive dynamics;
- emotionally exhausting UX.

Long-term retention should feel peaceful, not addictive.

---

## 7. Notifications & Reminder Strategy

Evaluate:
- emotional tone;
- frequency;
- timing;
- psychological safety;
- usefulness.

Detect:
- guilt-inducing notifications;
- anxiety-driven reminders;
- pressure mechanics;
- manipulative copy;
- spam risk;
- urgency framing.

Notifications should feel:
- respectful;
not demanding.

---

## 8. Psychological Safety

Evaluate whether the product avoids:
- shame;
- guilt;
- anxiety;
- pressure;
- compulsive usage patterns.

Detect:
- streak anxiety;
- fear-of-breaking-progress mechanics;
- “missed day” punishment;
- emotional dependency loops;
- productivity guilt.

Wellbeing products must never emotionally punish users.

---

## 9. Feature Fatigue Risk

Evaluate:
- complexity growth;
- onboarding overload;
- settings overload;
- metrics overload;
- cognitive maintenance burden.

Detect:
- feature creep;
- dashboard syndrome;
- excessive tracking;
- unnecessary personalization;
- emotional overload.

Retention decreases when maintenance cost grows.

---

## 10. Long-Term Emotional Attachment

Evaluate whether the product can realistically become:
- emotionally meaningful;
- trusted;
- comforting;
- personally valuable.

Review:
- emotional consistency;
- ritual feeling;
- intimacy;
- calmness;
- perceived care.

Detect:
- emotionally disposable UX;
- generic interactions;
- low emotional identity;
- weak attachment signals.

The strongest retention comes from emotional relevance.

- calm recurring value;
- emotional trust;
- healthy long-term retention.

Strongly damages trust, retention or emotional safety.

## High
Noticeably hurts onboarding quality or long-term engagement.

## Medium
Moderately impacts emotional continuity or retention.

## Low
Minor onboarding or retention improvement opportunity.
